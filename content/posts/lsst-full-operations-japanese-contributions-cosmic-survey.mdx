The NSF-DOE Vera C. Rubin Observatory has officially commenced full scientific operations for its ambitious 10-year Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST). This monumental undertaking aims to create an unprecedented record of the dynamic universe, observing changes in galaxies, stars, and solar system objects over time. Crucially, Japanese researchers and engineers, leveraging expertise from the Subaru Telescope, have played a pivotal role in supporting LSST's software, systems, and data management. This international collaboration is set to unlock profound insights into fundamental cosmic mysteries, from dark matter to the universe's evolution.

## What happened
Following its commissioning phase, the Rubin Observatory has now entered full scientific operations for the 10-year Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST). This survey will repeatedly observe the dynamic universe, creating an unparalleled record of how galaxies, stars, and solar system objects evolve. LSST is poised to address some of astronomy's biggest questions, including the nature of dark matter and dark energy, the universe's history, and the emerging field of multi-messenger astronomy through the discovery of transient events.

More than 80 Japanese researchers and engineers from institutions like the National Astronomical Observatory of Japan (NAOJ), the University of Tokyo, Chiba University, and Nagoya University are making significant contributions. Drawing on expertise gained from the Subaru Telescope's Hyper Suprime-Cam (HSC) project, they are involved in optimizing the LSST Camera for stable operations and leading the development of essential data visualization software. HSC observational data also served as a critical verification dataset to ensure a smooth start for LSST operations.

Japanese contributions extend to applying techniques for processing and managing large astronomical datasets, originally developed for HSC, to LSST. Additionally, they provide computing resources and technical support, which are vital for maintaining the computing environment that allows scientists worldwide to access and analyze LSST's enormous data archive. Many Japanese researchers have already secured data-access rights and are actively engaged in scientific analysis.

## Why it matters
The full operation of LSST represents a paradigm shift in astronomy, offering an unprecedented scope and depth of data that will transform our understanding of the cosmos. The sheer volume and cadence of observations will enable discoveries of transient events in numbers previously unimaginable, pushing the boundaries of multi-messenger astronomy and opening new avenues for research.

Of particular significance is the strong, complementary partnership between the Rubin Observatory and the Subaru Telescope. While LSST excels at wide-area surveys and identifying countless cosmic events, Subaru's advanced capabilities, such as its ʻŌnohiʻula Prime Focus Spectrograph (PFS), will provide detailed follow-up observations of these transient phenomena. This integrated approach maximizes scientific return, allowing researchers to capture the intricate details of cosmic events on a massive scale.

This international collaboration, involving Japan, the United States, and numerous other partners, underscores the global nature of modern big science. It ensures that the benefits of LSST's data and discoveries are shared and analyzed by a diverse scientific community, accelerating progress in understanding the universe's origin and evolution through a collective effort.

## How to think about it
Consider the LSST's full operation as a testament to the power of global scientific collaboration and technological synergy. It's not just about a single telescope, but a network of observatories and human expertise working in concert. Think of Rubin as the wide-field scout, identifying countless cosmic events, and Subaru as the detailed investigator, zooming in on the most intriguing phenomena. This division of labor, supported by shared data infrastructure and a global community of researchers, is the blueprint for tackling the most complex questions in astrophysics. It emphasizes that the future of astronomy lies in integrated, multi-instrument approaches that leverage diverse strengths.
